Introduction

Among the various gods and goddesses of the various mythologies of the world, my favorite set of myths are those of ancient Scandinavia: the Norse myths. Of the all the gods, goddesses, giants, trolls, elves, dwarves, and humans told of in the Norse myths Loki, the god of mischief, lord of the Jotuns, blood-brother of Odin, and father of monsters, stands out as one of the most complex and, to my way of thinking, characters.

Popular culture usually portrays Loki as evil, as it does with many of the trickster gods scattered about the world's myths. He was born, not of the main group of gods, the Aesir, but of the front-giants, the Jotuns (who are largely gruff, violent, destructive, rude, and ugly) and so is often convicted by guilt of association. Here, however, I would like to assert that, while Loki certainly does reprehensible, troublesome, and sometimes "evil" things, he is also the source of much good for both humanity and the gods. The trickster's archetypal role is that of instigator of change. This change, however, is not always asked for and not always pleasant.
